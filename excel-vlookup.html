Combining data in Excel

Bespoke Research | Sat 21 December 2019

Why did the Excel import the table? Because it was pivotal.

We all love spreadsheets, helps us do the calculations. Income, account receivables, expenses - anything you throw at it Excel will happily take and give you back results.

Sometimes you have a dataset that has a column, explanation for which is located in a different dataset. For example you have a list of business names along with NAICS code, but without NAICS code explanation, which tells you what industry is this business from. NAICS is 6 digit code and unless you work extensively with it, you won't remember what does it mean. You can always download full list of NAICS codes from Stats Canada and paste it in a separate sheet.

Fortunately you can easily get two datasets combined into one using handy VLOOKUP function.

Name NAICS
ABC Inc 334512
Acme Org 326160
Box Co 451120

[businesses]

Code Class Title
334512 Measuring, medical and controlling devices manufacturing
326160 Plastic bottle manufacturing
451120 Hobby, toy and game stores
...
[naics]

If you use the following formula in the cell C2 of the "businesses" sheet, you can get NAICS description conveniently listed next to each business name

=VLOOKUP(B2, naics!A:B, 2, FALSE);

Don't forget to double click on a small square in the bottom right corner of the cell, so that Excel can apply the formula to all cells in the row.

As a side note, this will work same way in Google Sheets as well.
